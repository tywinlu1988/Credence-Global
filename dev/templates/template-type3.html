1. Default Event Overview

Event Panorama

On November 10, 2020, Yongcheng Coal & Power Holding Group (AAA-rated provincial SOE) failed to repay the principal and interest of "20 Yongmei SCP003" amounting to 1.032 billion yuan, triggering one of the most severe shocks in the history of China's credit bond market. As Henan's largest state-owned coal enterprise (total assets of 172.4 billion yuan), Yongmei's default directly shattered the market's faith in "AAA SOE rigid repayment," causing a sharp decline in credit bond market liquidity: Within a week, yields on multiple coal enterprise/city investment bonds surged 200-500bp, credit bond issuance virtually halted, and many institutions suspended pledged repo transactions. More thought-provoking is that half a year before the default, Yongmei still maintained its AAA issuer rating — the external rating agencies' early warning capability for this event was nearly zero.

Event Background and Market Environment

The full name of Yongmei Holding is Yongcheng Coal & Power Holding Group Co., Ltd., a core subsidiary of the Henan provincial SOE Henan Energy & Chemical Group (Yuneng Chemical). Yongmei is primarily engaged in coal mining, washing, and sales, while also involved in non-coal businesses such as chemicals, trading, and equipment manufacturing. As of the end of 2019, the company had total assets of 172.4 billion yuan, annual revenue of approximately 48 billion yuan, and 57,000 employees, making it the largest coal enterprise in Henan Province.

Before the default occurred, China's credit bond market had long held a "SOE rigid repayment faith" — the widespread belief that AAA-rated state-owned enterprises would not default. This belief stemmed from the extremely rare cases of SOE defaults historically, and the fact that governments typically avoided public market defaults by coordinating financial institutions and injecting assets. Yongmei's default completely shattered this belief, marking a historic turning point in the pricing system of China's credit bond market. After the default, the market began to reprice SOE credit, the credit spread between weak and strong SOEs systematically widened, and the "SOE faith" premium was significantly compressed.

L3 Financial Strength: Deep Dive Analysis

The L3 score of only 3.5 (BB- equivalent) provided a key anchor for the framework's early warning. In-depth analysis reveals:

Liquidity Gap
Unrestricted monetary funds/short-term debt = 0.45x, far below the framework's safety threshold of 0.8x. Including unused bank credit facilities (~38 billion), the coverage ratio would be about 2.1x, but bank credit can be revoked at any time (especially during economic downturns). The "unrestricted monetary funds" metric used by the framework at the L3 level is much stricter than the "monetary funds" metric used by external rating agencies — after deducting restricted portions such as bank acceptance bill deposits, time deposit pledges, and escrow accounts, the actual freely usable funds are significantly reduced.
Short-Term Debt Rollover Pressure
Of the 105 billion yuan in interest-bearing debt, short-term debt was 65 billion (62%), with an average remaining maturity of about 9 months. TTM operating cash flow of about 6.9 billion yuan could only cover approximately 10% of short-term debt rollover needs. Once the market tightened, short-term debt could not be renewed. The framework's short-term debt ratio threshold is set at 55%; Yongmei's actual value of 65% far exceeded this threshold, triggering a red warning signal at the L3 level.
Parent Company Fund Depletion
The parent company effectively served as the group fund concentration center but retained only 12.4 billion yuan in monetary funds. The other 24.8 billion yuan in cash from the consolidated statements was distributed across subsidiaries (approximately 60% restricted), and the parent company's independent debt repayment capacity was severely overestimated. This discrepancy (12.4 billion vs 24.8 billion) was a core finding of the framework's dual-track analysis.
Key Findings Summary from L3 Financial Deep Dive: Yongmei appeared to have ample monetary funds at the consolidated level, but the parent company's liquidity position was far weaker than what the consolidated statements suggested. This "ample at consolidated level, tight at parent level" phenomenon is a common pitfall in credit analysis of group enterprises. By mandatorily comparing the differences between the two reporting perspectives, the framework effectively broke through this accounting information barrier.

Half-Year Change Deep Dive: Why Was the Financial Deterioration So Severe?

Epidemic Impact Amplifier
The COVID-19 outbreak in 2020Q1 caused a cliff-like decline in coal demand. Yongmei's main customers (thermal power plants, steel mills) had operating rates reduced to 60-70%, sales volume declined 25% YoY, payment collection cycles extended from 60 days to 90-120 days, directly causing a 62% contraction in operating cash flow. Henan Province's 2020 Q1 GDP was -6.8% YoY, severely impacting the economic fundamentals.
Funds Withdrawn by Parent Group
At the Henan Energy & Chemical Group level, greater debt repayment pressure led to large-scale fund transfers from Yongmei through the internal fund concentration system. Parent company monetary funds fell from 12.4 billion to 4.8 billion (-61%), while consolidated monetary funds only declined 19% (from 26.1 billion to 21.2 billion), indicating increased intensity of centralized fund deployment by the group. This validated the framework's L4 level assessment regarding "intra-group fund concentration risks."
External Financing Channels Narrowed
In the first half of 2020, affected by Yongmei's own negative signals and the overall industry credit contraction, bank credit facilities were compressed from 120 billion to approximately 95 billion yuan, with unused facilities dropping from 38 billion to about 20 billion. Bond market financing costs rose from below 4% to over 5%. Multiple outstanding bonds in the interbank market could not be rolled over upon maturity; in the first half of 2020, approximately 12 billion yuan of Yongmei's bonds matured, of which about 3 billion could not be repaid with new bonds and had to rely on own funds.

Key Technical Differences Where Framework Leads External Ratings:
(1) Consolidated vs Parent Dual-Track Analysis: The framework mandates simultaneous analysis of both consolidated and parent company statements, while external ratings primarily focus on consolidated statements. The difference between Yongmei's parent company monetary funds of 4.8 billion vs consolidated 26.1 billion revealed the truth about fund restrictions and group deployment.
(2) Unrestricted Monetary Fund Coverage Ratio: The framework set this indicator at L3 Financial level, penetrating the inflated "monetary funds" account. Calculated under the framework's methodology, Yongmei's unrestricted monetary funds/short-term debt was only 0.28x, far beyond external rating agencies' liquidity assessment.
(3) Related Party Transaction Penetration: The framework's L4 Governance level systematically tracks the scale of related party fund flows; Yongmei's 20 billion+ related party transactions with Yuneng Chemical were flagged as "high-risk penetration signals" — this carried almost no weight in external rating reports.
(4) True Ceiling of External Support: The framework does not accept the "SOE faith" assumption, but instead quantifies the upper limit of support capacity through objective indicators such as fiscal self-sufficiency rate, historical rescue cases, and intra-group rescue priority order.
(5) Audit Opinion Penetration: At the L4 level, the framework analyzes the "Key Audit Matters" section of the audit report, identifying auditors' implicit concerns about going concern, while external rating agencies typically only focus on the type of audit opinion (unqualified/qualified/adverse).

B. SOE Framework Weighting Basis

The framework sets a differentiated level weighting system for state-owned enterprises (especially provincial/municipal SOEs), with the core principles as follows:

(1) L3 Financial Strength carries the highest weight (30%): Regardless of the nature of the enterprise, financial data is the most objective indicator of default risk. Although SOEs theoretically enjoy government support, historical default cases show that when financial deterioration reaches a critical point, government support is often insufficient to cover all debts. Giving the highest weight to financial strength ensures the framework is not disturbed by the "SOE faith."

(2) L4 Management & Governance weight (20%, higher than general enterprises): Governance risks such as related party transactions, fund concentration, and insider control are more prominent in SOEs. The Yongmei case shows that governance deficiencies are often leading indicators of financial deterioration. L4 weight is increased from 15% in the general framework to 20%.

(3) L5 External Support weight (15%, but subject to preconditions): External support rating is not an automatic bonus. The framework stipulates that L5 will only give a positive score when the following three preconditions are met: (i) Local government fiscal self-sufficiency rate ≥60%; (ii) Historical rescue cases show the government has substantive rescue willingness and capacity; (iii) The issuer has irreplaceability in the local economy (employment/taxation/industrial chain). In the Yongmei case, none of the three conditions were fully met, and the L5 score was neutral to low.

(4) L1 Macro weight (15%), L2 Industry weight (20%): The weights of these two levels are consistent with the general framework, but in the SOE framework, additional attention is given to the impact of regional financial ecology (such as local bank non-performing loan ratio, regional financing environment).

C. Consolidated vs Parent Statement Difference Treatment Method

The Yongmei case revealed the critical impact of differences between consolidated and parent company statements on credit assessment. Framework v2.0 introduces the following treatment rules:

Rule 1 - Dual-Track Mandatory Analysis: All ratings must simultaneously analyze consolidated and parent company statements, and the scores of the two are not interchangeable. The consolidated statement reflects the overall asset size of the group, while the parent company statement reflects the actual solvency of the direct debt servicing entity. When the score difference between the two levels exceeds 1 point, the lower score is automatically taken as the final score.

Rule 2 - Differential Warning Mechanism: When the following indicators simultaneously trigger a differential warning, automatically downgrade by one notch: (i) Parent monetary funds/consolidated monetary funds <50%; (ii) Parent revenue/consolidated revenue <30%; (iii) Parent total assets/consolidated total assets <40%. In the Yongmei case, all three indicators were triggered.

Rule 3 - "Double Counting" Deduction: For intra-group transactions and fund flows between parent and subsidiaries, the framework identifies and deducts the "double counting" portion. A typical example is: Subsidiary A's monetary funds are simultaneously included in the consolidated monetary funds and the parent company's other receivables (through internal settlement accounts), leading to an overestimation of consolidated monetary funds.

Rule 4 - Minority Interest Adjustment: When minority interests account for more than 30% of net assets, the framework conducts separate analysis of debt attributable to minority shareholders to avoid consolidated debt being diluted by minority shareholder assets masking repayment pressure. In the Yongmei case, minority interests accounted for approximately 25% of net assets, close to the watch threshold.

13. Post-Event Reflection: Lessons and Practical Significance of Yongmei Default

Core Lessons

Lessons for Investors:
(1) Do not blindly rely on external ratings, especially rating bubbles involving "SOE faith"
(2) Must penetrate consolidated statements to analyze parent-level actual debt repayment capacity
(3) The "Monetary Funds" account requires subtraction — restricted funds, related party occupation, and group concentration all weaken actual liquidity

Lessons for Regulators/Rating Agencies:
(1) The rating industry's "issuer-pay" model requires fundamental reform to reduce rating shopping incentives
(2) SOE rating methodology should cancel automatic government support premiums and replace with conditional trigger-based assessment
(3) Consolidated and parent statements should mandatorily require dual-track rating disclosure
(4) Monetary fund restriction status should be mandatory disclosure content in bond prospectuses and rating reports
(5) Establish a "Rating Bubble Index" as an auxiliary tool for market risk early warning

Practical Significance for Current Markets (2026 Perspective)

It has been nearly 6 years since Yongmei's default, and its profound lessons still have important implications for the current credit bond market:

(1) Persistent Fading of "SOE Faith": After Yongmei, a series of risk events emerged from Huarong, Evergrande, Country Garden, Sino-Ocean and other SOEs/large private enterprises; the pricing logic of the credit bond market has shifted from "faith-driven" to "fundamentals-driven." (2) Gradual Nature of Rating Industry Reform: Although regulators have promoted a series of rating industry reforms after Yongmei's default (such as canceling mandatory ratings, introducing dual ratings, strengthening rating disclosure), rating inflation still exists. As of 2026, the degree of credit spread differentiation among AAA-rated enterprises is far higher than what ratings can reflect, indicating that rating differentiation remains insufficient.

New modules have been added, including ESG scoring dimensions, supply chain financial risk assessment, and regional financial ecology indices. (4) Long-Term Value of Early Warning Framework: In a market environment where credit bond defaults have become normalized and rating differentiation is insufficient, the value of an early warning framework independent of external ratings is increasingly prominent for investors. The framework does not assume any entity is "too big to fail" and does not favor any type of issuer based on enterprise nature — this independence was fully validated in the Yongmei case and will continue to play a role in future risk events.
