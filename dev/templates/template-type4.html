I. Assessment Background

Huachen Automotive Group Overview

Huachen Automotive Group Holdings Co., Ltd. ("Huachen Automotive") is a large-scale state-owned automobile enterprise group under the Liaoning provincial government, with its ultimate controller being Liaoning SASAC. Its core subsidiaries include: Huachen China (01114.HK, holding 50% of Huachen BMW equity, contributing approximately RMB 5 billion in annual profits), Jinbei Automotive Co., Ltd. (600609.SH, A-share listed commercial vehicle enterprise), Huachen Renault (light commercial vehicle joint venture), and Zhonghua Auto (indigenous passenger car brand). The group employs approximately 42,000 people, making it one of the largest industrial enterprises in Liaoning Province.

As of the end of June 2020, the group's consolidated total assets were approximately RMB 64.8 billion, with an asset-liability ratio of 78%, a current ratio of 0.92, and negative operating net cash flow in the first half of the year (-RMB 1.2 billion). There were 17 outstanding bonds with a total balance of RMB 4.5 billion, of which short-term bonds maturing/puttable within one year accounted for approximately 60%. The assessment date was approximately 4.5 months before the actual default event (November 16, 2020, "19 Huachen MTN001" failed to pay interest on schedule). Notably, the financial indicators at the parent company (bond issuer) level were significantly weaker than the consolidated statements — the parent company's asset-liability ratio was 93%, and its cash-to-short-term-debt ratio was only 0.31x, indicating a severe "headquarters hollowing-out" risk, where high-quality assets (BMW JV equity) were concentrated at the subsidiary level, while liabilities were concentrated at the parent company level.

Background and Purpose of This Assessment

The same credit entity presents a distinctly different credit profile under different business perspectives. Credit approval officers focus on collateral coverage and government support, bond investors focus on secondary market price signals and liquidity, and portfolio risk control focuses on concentration risk and tail events. This report, through parallel assessment of three roles, reveals the impact of perspective differences on credit judgment, providing a methodological basis for the framework's multi-role adaptation.

Core Question: When the same entity shows systematic scoring differences under three role perspectives (in this case, a variance of 2.4 points, approximately 3-4 rating sub-levels), how should judgments be reconciled? Which perspective is closest to the actual outcome? What are the sources of divergence? Through an ex-post analysis of Huachen's 2020 default, this report attempts to answer these questions, providing empirical evidence for iterative framework optimization.

Why Huachen Automotive as the Case Study: Huachen's default is a landmark event in China's credit market — it is a Liaoning provincial SOE holding high-quality core assets (BMW JV), but ultimately defaulted due to liquidity depletion at the parent company level and insufficient government rescue capacity. The complexity of this case makes it an ideal test scenario for multi-role parallel assessment. The different judgment logics of the three roles (collateral optimism vs market pessimism vs risk aversion) were most fully demonstrated in this case.

Multi-Role Assessment Methodology Overview

Unified pyramid framework (L1-L5) as the underlying data structure, with the three roles sharing five layers of foundational data: macro, industry, financial, governance, and external support. Differences manifest in three dimensions: risk weight allocation (M0 emphasizes collateral/L5, M1 emphasizes L2/L3/L5, M4 emphasizes risk floor), time horizon (M0 annually, M1 quarterly, M4 monthly), and risk tolerance (M0 moderate, M1 cautious, M4 extremely low).

Credit Approval Decision Logic

The credit approval perspective naturally focuses more on collateral coverage and government support. Huachen BMW JV contributes approximately RMB 5 billion in annual profits (via Huachen China 01114.HK dividends), serving as a stable primary repayment source. The Shenyang factory land + equipment collateral is assessed at approximately RMB 8 billion, covering the exposure by approximately 1.5x. The Liaoning provincial SOE status means that government coordination would intervene in extreme scenarios.
Decision Recommendation: Maintain existing credit facilities (no new exposure), require quarterly collateral revaluation reports, monitor sales data changes after auto shows. It is recommended to simultaneously monitor Huachen China 01114.HK's stock price and dividend payment records as tracking indicators for the primary repayment source.
Potential Blind Spots: The credit approval perspective overestimates the certainty of government support and the recovery rate of collateral in bankruptcy proceedings. Liaoning's fiscal self-sufficiency rate is only 42%, and government relief cannot legally guarantee full repayment. Additionally, among the collateral assessed at RMB 8 billion, industrial land and specialized equipment may experience 40-60% discount in bankruptcy auctions.

M0 Credit Approval Scoring Logic Detailed Explanation:
L1 score 5.5 (highest) stems from Huachen's strategic position as the leading auto enterprise in Liaoning Province, with clear demands from the provincial party committee and government for its stable operations, and the overall regional credit environment being controllable. L2 score 4.0 (moderately high) is based on the "BMW JV as safety net" judgment — although indigenous brand sales in H1 2020 declined 35% YoY, the BMW China JV contributed approximately 80% of the group's profits, sufficient to cover bank loan interest. L3 score 4.0 collateral coverage calculation: Shenyang factory land (book value RMB 3.5B) + machinery equipment (book value RMB 2.8B) + other real estate (book value RMB 1.7B), totaling RMB 8B book value, covering RMB 6B credit exposure at a 75% loan-to-value ratio. L4 score 3.5 reflects a prudent assessment of SOE governance efficiency, but considers it "will not directly affect debt repayment capacity." L5 score 5.0 is based on Liaoning SASAC's historical zero-tolerance attitude toward provincial SOE debt defaults, but from a methodological perspective, this judgment relies excessively on qualitative assumptions rather than quantitative fiscal capacity.

M0 Decision Implicit Assumptions: (1) Collateral value will maintain over 70% of assessed value in default; (2) Liaoning provincial government will coordinate provincial SOEs for rescue in extreme scenarios; (3) BMW JV annual dividends will not be interrupted; (4) Banks can avoid triggering default through loan rollovers. These four assumptions were all disproven to varying degrees post-hoc — collateral auction discount exceeded expectations, government coordination failed, BMW JV dividends were frozen after default, and loan rollovers could not be executed under cross-default clauses.

Bond Investment Decision Logic

The bond investment perspective focuses more on secondary market price signals and liquidity. Huachen had 17 outstanding bonds with a balance of RMB 4.5 billion, with the ChinaBond valuation yield rising from 5.5% at the beginning of 2020 to over 8.5%. Institutional investors had already begun reducing positions in the secondary market, and although trading remained sparse, the direction was clear. Bond holders are general unsecured creditors, with a lower priority in the repayment hierarchy than secured bank loans — this is a fundamental difference from the credit approval perspective.
Decision Recommendation: Reduce existing bond positions by over 50%, retain only liquid short-duration instruments. Suspend all primary market subscriptions entirely. Set a daily stop-loss line of 200bp for market maker accounts. Monitor maturity payment announcements for short-term bonds such as 19 Huachen MTN001 and 19 Huachen 02, and set valuation alert thresholds.
Signal Confirmation: The movement of ChinaBond valuation yield from 5.5% to 8.5% is a forward-looking indicator of market perception of credit risk. The yield curve had already begun to flatten at the short end, indicating increased market pricing of repayment uncertainty. From July to September 2020, three block sell orders totaling approximately RMB 420 million appeared, reflecting large institutions accelerating their liquidation.

M1 Bond Investment Scoring Logic Detailed Explanation:
L1 score 5.0 is lower than M0's 5.5 because bond investors focus more on the regional economy's macro debt repayment capacity — Liaoning Province's Q1 2020 GDP growth of -8.2% was worse than the national average (-6.8%), but the fiscal self-sufficiency rate of only 42% means provincial fiscal capacity imposes objective constraints on large SOE rescues. L2 score 3.0 reflects a pessimistic judgment on indigenous brands — Huachen Zhonghua's full-year 2019 sales were only 25,000 vehicles, with a YoY decline of over 60%, meaning the brand had effectively exited mainstream market competition. L3 score 2.5 is the most critical warning signal: at the parent company level, H1 2020 operating cash flow net outflow was RMB 1.2 billion, cash balance approximately RMB 3 billion, but short-term debt exceeded RMB 7.1 billion, with a short-term debt coverage ratio of only 0.42x. L4 score 3.0 focuses on a key signal — the auditor added a "material uncertainty related to going concern" paragraph for the first time, a quantitative warning to which bond investors are highly sensitive. L5 score 3.5 reflects an ambivalent mindset: believing in government willingness but not in the government's capacity for full rescue.

M1 Decision Unique Perspective Supplement: An important advantage of the bond investment perspective over credit approval is that bond prices provide a continuous, objective "market voting mechanism." From January to June 2020, Huachen's ChinaBond valuation yield consistently rose from 5.5% to 8.5%, an objective hard signal independent of analyst subjective judgment. During the same period, M0 credit approval ratings did not change at all, indicating that rating agency adjustment speed was far slower than the market price signal generation speed. Additionally, bond investors noted a structural feature — 11 of Huachen's 17 outstanding bonds had cross-default clauses, meaning any single bond default would accelerate all debt maturity, a legal clause-level risk completely overlooked in M0's collateral assessment.

Portfolio Risk Control Decision Logic

The portfolio risk control perspective is naturally more conservative, focusing on tail risk and concentration. Huachen's credit spread in the portfolio had already breached the 99% VaR threshold, with portfolio concentration reaching 3.2% (exceeding the 2% single-entity limit). More importantly, a Huachen default would generate second-order contagion effects, impacting other auto industry and Liaoning provincial SOE bonds within the same portfolio. This perspective is inherently more conservative — better to err on the side of caution than to miss the risk.
Decision Recommendation: Immediately initiate a reduction plan, reduce Huachen exposure to below 1% of portfolio within 30 days. Suspend all Huachen-related transactions. Initiate alternative asset allocation plans, focus on comparable auto industry targets such as JAC and Foton. Simultaneously reduce the concentration of Liaoning provincial SOE bonds in the portfolio to prevent systemic contagion.
Second-Order Effect Simulation: If Huachen defaults, other auto industry bonds in the portfolio are expected to widen by 150-200bp, Liaoning provincial SOE bonds by 80-120bp. Portfolio NAV estimated drawdown of 2.8%. At the 95% confidence level, the maximum portfolio drawdown is estimated at 4.2%, exceeding the portfolio risk control hard limit of 3.5%.

M4 Portfolio Risk Control Scoring Logic Detailed Explanation:
L1 score 4.0 is only moderate because portfolio risk control uses a "worst-case assumption" framework — assuming the most adverse regional economic scenario (Liaoning GDP growth -15%, fiscal revenue -20%), under which Huachen's credit support would significantly weaken. L2 score 2.0 is based on "worst-case industry scenario assumptions" — auto industry full-year 2020 sales expected to decline 15-20%, with indigenous brands (Zhonghua/Jinbei) potentially facing market clearance in 2021. L3 score 1.5 is the lowest among the three roles, with the key being the assessment of "parent company level cash flow": although consolidated statements show positive operating cash flow, the parent company (borrower/issuer entity) level already exhibits "hematopoietic function failure," with operating cash net outflow of RMB 1.2 billion, relying entirely on Huachen China dividends to maintain surface liquidity. L4 score 2.0 focuses on agency cost issues in governance structure — as a wholly-owned enterprise of Liaoning SASAC, Huachen Automotive's management decisions are subject to significant administrative intervention, with limited market-oriented risk disposal capacity. L5 score 1.0 is the most forward-looking judgment: through quantitative fiscal analysis, Liaoning Province's available fiscal resources in 2020 were approximately RMB 250 billion, while the scale of SOE debt requiring rescue during the same period exceeded RMB 20 billion (including Huachen's RMB 4.5B bonds and approximately RMB 6B in bank loans), making it difficult to avoid partial debt defaults even with full provincial government coordination.

M4 Decision Framework Distinctive Methodology: The portfolio risk control perspective uses a "worst-case scenario planning approach" rather than a "most likely scenario assessment approach." Specifically: (1) It does not make assumptions about the "reasonable level" of credit spreads, but directly assesses the objective fact that "spreads have already breached the historical 99th percentile"; (2) It does not use consolidated statement data, but takes the parent company (direct counterparty) financials as the assessment baseline — parent company asset-liability ratio 93%, cash-to-short-term-debt ratio 0.31x, operating cash flow negative for 2 consecutive years; (3) It introduces second-order effect analysis — the spillover effect of a Huachen default on other auto bonds and Liaoning provincial SOE bonds in the portfolio; (4) It uses a "stressed VaR" framework to replace traditional parametric VaR — stressed VaR assumes correlation coefficients rise by 50% during crises, better capturing the non-linear characteristics of tail events. Although this conservative methodology may lead to "false positives" (misjudging risk), it was validated as the correct judgment framework in the Huachen case.

Time Series Core Findings:

Methodological Implications of Time Series for Multi-Role Assessment

Time series analysis reveals an important dynamic characteristic of multi-role assessment — the temporal evolution pattern of divergence:

Phase 1 (T-12 to T-6): Divergence Formation Period. During this phase, the three roles' base information converges (all are public financial data), but differences in each role's risk preference begin to manifest, with variance expanding from 1.2 to 2.4 pts. M4 is the first to downgrade (from 4.0 to 1.8), while M0 only makes minor adjustments (from 5.2 to 4.2). Divergence in this phase is primarily driven by risk preference.

Phase 2 (T-6 to T-3): Divergence Acceleration Period. Credit deterioration signals increase (YTM surge, audit warning, block sell orders), M1 and M4 respond quickly, but M0 still tends to interpret negative information as "short-term fluctuations." Variance expands from 2.4 to its peak of 2.6 pts. Divergence in this phase is primarily driven by differences in information response speed.

Phase 3 (T-3 to Default): Divergence Convergence Period. When default becomes an open topic of discussion, M0 is forced to accelerate downgrades, and the three roles' cognition gradually converges. Variance narrows from 2.6 to 2.0 pts. Convergence in this phase is driven by objective facts.

Framework Implications: If the divergence expansion trend could be identified in Phase 1 (divergence formation period) and a "most conservative perspective stress test" initiated, credit deterioration signals could be detected earlier. It is recommended to add a "variance trend monitoring" module in Framework v3.0 — when the variance expansion rate from T-6 to T-3 exceeds 0.3 pts/month, automatically trigger an alert and notify the M0 Credit Approval role to reassess its assumptions.

Cross-Comparison Core Findings

Dimension cross-comparison reveals three core findings:

First, time preference differences drive divergence. M0's 1-year credit facility cycle makes it more concerned with "whether rollover is possible" (short-term rolling), M1's 3-6 month holding period focuses on "trading exit window," and M4's 1-3 month rebalancing cycle focuses on "immediate liquidation." Second, information view asymmetry. Information differences are not just about "how much," but more about "which dimension" — this leads to the largest judgment differences among the three roles on L3 Financial Strength and L5 External Support.

Third, repayment hierarchy determines risk exposure. As a secured lender, M0 has the highest priority in the legal repayment structure (actual recovery rate 40-55%). As bondholders (general unsecured creditors), M1 has the lowest repayment priority (actual recovery rate 12-20%). As a portfolio manager, M4 focuses on the overall portfolio net value and drawdown resilience, rather than individual entity recovery rate. For the same underlying asset, risk assessment conclusions should naturally differ due to the different legal nature of the holding instruments.

Bias Source 1: Optimism Bias. Bias Source 2: Information Lag Bias. The same pyramid framework, by adjusting risk tolerance parameters, time horizon parameters, and information weight parameters, adapts to different business role needs. - External Support Discount Rate (ESD): M0=0.9, M1=0.6, M4=0.2

Framework Improvement Insights

This multi-role assessment reveals that the framework needs to balance standardization (ensuring comparability) and personalization (adapting to business needs). It is recommended to introduce a "role adjustment matrix" in Framework v3.0, allowing differentiated scoring and rating outputs by role type based on the same underlying data.

v3.0 Framework Improvement Recommendations:
1) Establish a unified underlying data layer (L1-L5 raw scores shared)
2) Introduce a role adjustment layer (adjust weights and thresholds by M0/M1/M4)
3) Output layer retains perspective differences (differences are information, not to be concealed)
4) Add mandatory divergence analysis module (automatically triggered when variance >2.0)

Methodological Boundaries of This Assessment

This assessment validates the framework based on post-hoc data, but the following methodological limitations should be noted:

(1) Rearview Mirror Bias: Assessors at the 2020 time point could not fully predict the November 2020 default event; this report's post-hoc analysis takes advantage of "already knowing the outcome" information.
(2) Single Case Limitation: The analysis conclusions from the Huachen case may not apply to multi-role assessments of other industries or other types of enterprises.
(3) Role Stereotyping Risk: The M0/M1/M4 classification is a simplified model; in practice, opinion divergence also exists within each team.
(4) Scoring Subjectivity: L1-L5 scores at the 2020 time point were subjectively determined by analysts; different analysts under the same role perspective may also give different scores.
(5) Data Availability Differences: Some core data (such as BMW JV detailed financial statements, Liaoning provincial fiscal rescue budgets) were not publicly available at the 2020 time point.

Nevertheless, the core method of this report — multi-role parallel assessment — as an institutionalized risk perspective management tool, still has significant framework building value.

Methodology Summary and Framework Application Recommendations

This report systematically demonstrates the methodological framework and application value of multi-role parallel assessment through the Huachen Automotive case. The core methodology is summarized as follows:

First, the core value of multi-role assessment is not "finding the correct answer," but "revealing differences." In the Huachen case, the three-role score variance (2.4 pts) itself is the most important information — it indicates that the entity is in a "credit cognitive divergence zone," requiring more prudent risk management measures. Even without knowing which role is "correct," a variance exceeding 2.0 pts is itself a risk warning signal.

Second, the conservative perspective is more forward-looking in tail events. In this case, M4 Portfolio Risk Control's conservative assumptions were closest to the actual default outcome. But this does not mean conservatism is always correct — during credit expansion periods, excessive conservatism may lead to opportunity costs. The value of multi-role assessment lies precisely in simultaneously presenting three perspectives — optimistic, neutral, and conservative — allowing decision-makers to choose the appropriate anchor based on their own risk preferences and portfolio characteristics.

Third, the systemic roots of divergence require institutional solutions. Repayment hierarchy differences, time preference differences, and risk preference differences are objectively existing structural factors that should not be eliminated — they are manifestations of the value of different business roles. However, information asymmetry and methodology assumption differences can be reduced through institutional improvements, including establishing a "multi-role shared information platform" and a "unified key assumption library."

Fourth, the iteration direction for Framework v3.0 is clear. By introducing a "role adjustment matrix," quantifying external support scores, establishing an automatic variance alert mechanism, and enhancing the weight of market signals within the framework, Framework v3.0 will achieve a better balance between standardization and personalization.

Fifth, the Huachen case will become the benchmark case for multi-role assessment. This case covers core multi-role assessment issues including government support uncertainty, collateral value overestimation, parent company hollowing-out risk, and cross-default clause impact. Future new cases will be systematically compared against Huachen, gradually building a "case benchmark library" for multi-role assessment.
